How does judging work?
We will have a judging expo on Sunday ☀️ from 10:15 to 13:15, divided into three 1-hour time slots. To present your project, follow these steps:

Submit your project to Devpost before the deadline ⏳!
The organizers will send a message on Slack with your time slot.
When your turn is up, head to the front of the A3 building, where an organizer will add you to the queue 🏢
Wait for the organizer to call your project name and assign you your judging room 🙋
Go to that room and present your amazing project to the judges 🏆
Judging Criteria
Teams will be judged based on these three criteria, which judges will weigh equally. Participants should try to describe what they did for each criterion in their project.

Technology: How technically impressive was the hack? Was the technical problem the team dealt with difficult? Did it use a particularly clever technique or did it use many different components? Did the technology involved make you go “Wow”?
Idea & Innovation: How good is the idea? We want to see things that go beyond a website that already exists, so be creative!
Learning: Did the team stretch themselves? Did they try to learn something new? What kind of projects have they worked on before? If a team always does virtual reality projects and decides to switch up and try doing a mobile app instead, it should be rewarded. We will also take into account the design and usability, so if your application does not look like a 90's website, and it's intuitive when using it, extra points for you!
These criteria will guide judges, but ultimately, they are free to make decisions based on their gut feeling about which projects are the most impressive.

It's important to note that these judging criteria do not include:

How good your code is. It doesn't matter if your code is messy, not well commented, or uses inefficient algorithms. Hacking is about playing around, making mistakes, and learning new things. If your code isn't production-ready, we won’t mark you down.
How well you pitch. Hacking is about building and learning, not about selling.
How viable or world-changing your idea is. You can build something totally useless, but as long as you're learning and having fun, that's a good hack! Sometimes a pointless project is one of the best hacks, so don't worry about coming up with the next big idea or building the next Facebook. You'll have plenty of time for that outside the hackathon, so just focus on learning, having fun, and making new friends. At the end of the day, the skills you learn and the friends you make might lead to the next big thing - but you don't have to do that to win a hackathon.
Demos
After hacking finishes, teams present their projects to the judges.

You are highly encouraged to present a live demo of your work. While presentations or pitches are permitted, they are less preferred, and the use of slides is strictly prohibited. Remember, you will not be judged on the quality of your pitch, nor on the quality of the idea; you are judged on what you built, so the best thing to do is a demo.

You are encouraged to present even if your hack is broken, or you weren’t able to finish—that happens all the time! Completion is not part of the judging criteria, so don't worry about it not being finished. Demoing is not just about the competition, it's a chance to share with others what you learned and what you tried to build—that's what hacking's all about! For being brave enough to demo, you'll receive a special "I Demoed" sticker, no matter how it is! If you don't have anything to demo, you can present what you tried and what you learned. Hearing what other people learned is interesting and inspiring for all attendees.

Remember
The competition is just a part of the hackathon. To make the most out of the event, try something new, teach other people, and make new friends!
